Check Out Niche.com (191010)

​
​

Admission

​

Essay

​

​
Quoted from https://www.forbes.com/sites/willarddix/#370294455200

作者观点

I’ve been an associate dean of admission at Amherst College and college counselor at a well-known Chicago private school. For 12 years, I helped underserved students……

Call me perverse, but one of my favorite leisure activities is to perform dramatic readings of bad Yelp reviews. In general, the good ones are boring, pretentious and predictable; the bad ones are hilarious, idiosyncratic, usually brutally misspelled and more revealing of the writer than the restaurant or business being reviewed. Tolstoy’s maxim that “All happy families are alike; each unhappy family is unhappy in its own way” applies particularly well here. Once you’ve read one good review you’ve read them all, but the bad ones are delectable, each in its own right.

Going through the college research process can seem like reading only positive Yelp reviews. After a few chirpy campus tours and admission officer presentations, you kind of get the picture that there’s no “typical” student at Old Main; the campus is a “designated arboretum;” students “work hard and play hard;” you can form your own club if there isn’t one already; professors give out their home phone numbers; and everyone is tolerant. It’s a dream world where you can spend time studying under the trees or lounge in your luxe, be-kitchened co-ed dorm making brownies for all-night study sessions.

Reality, of course, generally presents itself quite differently. The website Niche.com focuses on helping people explore neighborhoods and schools (including K-12) where they might want to locate or relocate. It has a “Best Colleges in America” section you can check out with the usual grain or two of salt, but unlike the U.S. News rankings, it also has subsections including “Will You Get In?”, “After College” and, its Yelp-iest section, “Reviews.”

That section features comments by self-identified students, alums and employees of the school in question. (Caution: I registered on the site and submitted a review of my alma mater to see if they’d ask for some verification of my status but they didn’t.) Following my Yelp habit, I breezed past the positive reviews and checked out the “poor” and “terrible” reviews to see what I could learn.

As expected, students seemed most often disappointed in the athletics, food, social life, parking, weather and living conditions. Some were resigned to their fate, others were bitter about their choice. But as with Yelpers, comments usually revealed more about the writer than the institution. Some samples of one- and two-star comments with my comments in italics:

​

引用Niche.com例子

Pomona College (CA): “Don’t Expect Much – Don’t expect to come to this school expecting to have a typical, blackout-filled college experience. While there are some groups of students who wish they went to a more fratty college, and act like it, this school has a pretty subdued nightlife.” — I’ve never realized that the typical college experience was “blackout-filled.”

“We are a Division III school, so our sports aren’t very good. Our most talented competitive organization is the Claremont Colleges Ballroom Dance Company, and our most talented sport is men’s water polo. Pomona is a college of brainiacs rather than jocks, though, and I don’t think many people actually care about our sports teams.” — This was a one-star review by someone who seems disappointed that a Division III college is a Division III college. It’s like the Yelp reviewer who complains that a BBQ restaurant has no vegetarian options.

Tulane University (LA): “Tulane’s core requirements waste too much time on arts and humanities and not enough of mathematics and sciences. There is no “Tulane culture” to speak of, in fact, the only thing that graduates might find unifying is waking up next to one another after a night of binge drinking; that is if they hadn’t been mugged on the way to their dorms.” — This from a junior who’s clearly more focused on getting through college than enjoying it and who doesn’t have a very good opinion of New Orleans.

“Lived in Monroe. Small, loud, stinky, old, absolutely disgusting. Over $7k to live in a double. Puke and urine all over everything. Used condoms on the floodlights. You name it. Super social and fun. But gets old FAST.” — I’d imagine so.

Carleton College (MN): “People are accepting but diversity is pretty limited. 10% international students means, what? 50 kids a year right. It’s Minnesota so white is to be expected, but pretty much everyone here is from the ‘burbs.’ Ethnic diversity is kind of sad but understandable. There are a lot of different political beliefs, but they aren’t explicit. Everyone here is accepted no matter who they are or where they come from, there’s pretty much a group on campus for everything, but that doesn’t stop diversity from being not great.” — So there’s a lot of difference, but not a lot of “difference.”

“Over All [sic] Experience – So far I’m not having the best time here. The academics are great. But other than that the town is pretty boring, there isn’t great diversity, and you feel very isolated there’s definitely a “Carleton bubble”. This school probably isn’t for me.” — A freshman discovering why good research is important.

Yale University (CT): “The food quality claims to be wonderful, but the bread can be stale or even moldy. The food program is mandatory during some years, and it is always overpriced. There are no options outside of the dining hall system. Unlike some campuses, there are no alternative restaurants or food places where one might use meal points or card swipes. Definitely not worth the money or the hassle.” — Makes Yale sound absolutely Dickensian.

“Distinctly Underwhelming – For all the fuss about the quality of Yale’s housing, I simply don’t rate it. Freshman dorms are universally mediocre to poor; expect to be sharing a bathroom with six or seven others, and expect outdated rooms that haven’t been refurbished in years…[T]here are far too many doubles throughout. Many justify the housing by saying that other places have it worse; I don’t doubt that, but that doesn’t make Yale’s facilities good. If Yale stopped pretending that its housing was fantastic, perhaps it could improve it. Living environment is integral to personal happiness, and for over $55,000 a year I simply expect better.” — A very disappointed freshman who clearly never had to share a room with a sibling while growing up.

Unsurprisingly, each of these institutions has many times more “excellent/very good/average” reviews than “poor/terrible” ones. But reading the bad reviews should inspire high school students and parents to dig deeper into the issues they reveal. Take them with a healthy dose of skepticism; remember that a student who thinks the typical college experience is “blackout-filled” may not be the most reliable critic (remember The Girl on the Train?) and you may get some useful dimensionality to the all-too-sunny depictions of colleges in viewbooks, on websites and at presentations.

IB English HL — How to study with JSTOR Text Analyzer (191106）

English HL

Article recommended

JSTOR Text Analyzer provides students with an additional resource for finding scholarly material

Quoted from Rachel Herrmann April 25, 2019

In teaching History in Practice, I’ve used JSTOR Text Analyzer, a research tool built by JSTOR Labs, to help students hone their researching and editing abilities. The easiest way to describe Text Analyzer is that it lets you upload files you’ve read or are working on, then uses the data in this file to provide additional reading recommendations.

Student Assignments Using Text Analyzers

Quoted fron one of teachers who plan and use JSTOR text analyzer

There’s a seminar where students read the article that their seminar tutor assigns, and then participate in an in-class assignment I’ve designed called “The Wandering Essay” to work out the article’s argument. And there’s a seminar where students have to locate additional reading related to that first article, with an eye toward building a bibliography. And lastly there’s a seminar where students bring an essay outline and introduction to class and workshop it with their peers.

That second seminar, where students have to locate an additional relevant reading, is where JSTOR Text Analyzer comes in. Library search catalogues can be difficult for first year students (and sometimes their seminar tutors) to navigate, and JSTOR Text Analyzer provides students with an additional resource for finding and thinking about scholarly material.
I set students up for this second seminar by giving a lecture about the department’s expectations for their essays—I discuss essay structure, writing, and editing suggestions, and the grading criteria we’ll use to assess them. I talk about reading footnotes to find related work. Then, I do a demonstration of how JSTOR Text Analyzer works. I upload the article that I teach to my seminar group, on cannibalism in colonial Jamestown. (N.B.: I conducted these searches while logged into my university’s library catalogue, so your results might look a little different.) Here’s what you get when you put my article through the Text Analyzer:

As you can see, my article on cannibalism in colonial Virginia gets tagged with topics on food history, United States history, British literature, American literature, and human cannibalism. Students might increase the importance of topics on literature (and point out that I’ve read my sources more like a historian than a literary scholar). They might increase the importance of articles on cannibalism to explain how another scientist or historian has made an argument about cannibalism that differs significantly from mine.

Building Syllabi with Text Analyzer##

As for the Text Analyzer’s usefulness for scholars, I have found it a helpful starting point in some of my own teaching and research. You can upload your syllabi onto the site and see what happens. I uploaded mine on Native American history. The Analyzer results mean that I can see many of the topics I assigned:

Because the Analyzer included all of my extra text about ways to succeed in the module, there’s a bit too much emphasis in the search results on finding sources. I can therefore increase the importance of prioritized terms:

I can add my own terms, too. I think I’d like it even more if I could add in additional authors to address the Analyzer’s current omissions. In other words, this is a good place to start, but it’s not where I’d end if you’re trying to diversify your curriculum.

Is JSTOR Text Analyzer a flawless tool? No. But it offers teachers a new way to show students research strategies. My students need that guidance on finding appropriate material, and they need to be encouraged to find that material early on in the semester. If it helps them to begin thinking about how material gets digitized, catalogued, and displayed, even better.
