Maths SL IA —— What matters in your IA report? (190929)

Maths SL

Exploration (IA)

You don’t need to make your IA topic very complicated and innovative in order to achieve a good grade in a subject

Quoted from reddit “PSA: you don’t need to make your IA topic very complicated and innovative…”

I was talking about IAs with some IB students not long ago, and this made me remember something that I wanted to tell to people on this sub for some time now, and something that I wish I could tell to myself and my classmates 1 year ago. Basically, it’s the title: you don’t absolutely have to choose some kind of super interesting and complex topic for your IA if you are aiming for a good grade.

At around the same time one year ago, I was stressing out about my chemistry, biology and mathematics IAs, trying to find some original idea that would blow away the examiner and score me the top mark. A lot of things were happening at that point in time, both in life and in school, that made me feel extremely exhausted and stressed. In the end, I couldn’t be bothered anymore, and settled for something very simple and overdone in every subject. For my chemistry IA, I did a vitamin C titration (as the other 50% of my class), for my biology IA, I did an experiment about the effects of pH on yeast activity, and for my math IA, I modelled an infectious disease using the SIR model. If you have any experience with these subjects and if you have researched IA topics already, you probably know that these 3 topics are probably some of the most overdone topics in IB history. I thought that I was committing a grave mistake and had to give up my hope for those sweet 7s, but I couldn’t really do anything about my chosen topics anymore and had to go with it. In return, I made sure that I made my IAs as good as possible (religiously followed each criterium word by word, followed all the tips I could find on the internet, asked people here and on the discord for help if I was stuck, etc). And finally, I also tried my best to perform well in the exams. In the end, I managed to score 7s in both biology and chemistry HL, and was 1 mark away from a 7 in math SL. And mind you, I was not some kind of top student who scored 100/100 on exams. I performed maybe above average on the exams, and even then, my super basic and overdone IAs did not hinder me from achieving a good grade.

I hope that this does not come out as bragging, because that’s not what I’m trying to achieve here. I just wanted to demonstrate that it’s okay to go for an easy IA topic, perform well in the exams, and still get good grades, without having to put yourself through hell to find this super-amazing-IA-idea-that-will-blow-everyone’s-mind-away. I saw a lot of classmates try to do that and stress themselves out a lot about their IA topic. Eventually, because they would choose an overly complicated topic, it would inevitably fail/they would come over a blunder that was hard to solve, and they had to start over again, which added on to their already stressful lives. Basically, there’s nothing wrong with choosing an easy and overdone topic. If you’re super passionate and interested in an idea, then go for it! But if your schedule is tight and you’re stressed out already, then know that it’s okay to settle for something nice and easy :) Just make sure to choose a topic that yields itself to the criteria easily, and to follow the criteria word by word - then you should not have any problem achieving a good grade!

Agree. What matters is your report. Having a detailed analysis of your data after processing and evaluating your hypothesis and research question insightfully (for bio: limitations and improvements, inherent weaknesses) is sufficient to perform well in these criteria. Having a concise background with relevant information related to your IA is what your markers look for.

Comments

Way too many people think criteria like personal engagement is based on how interesting/unique the topic of investigation is. This is untrue. Personal engagement is based on how you tailor your experiment and data collection methodology to accommodate whatever resources and equipment you have, and to answer your research question. In other words, explain why you performed the non-standard steps in your procedure, explain difficulties and modifications you made to allow the experiment to work, write as if you didn’t half-ass it (even if you did).
